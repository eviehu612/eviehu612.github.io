Plato's Dilemma of Fiction

December 11th, 2025

The form of the Republic initially appears to contradict its content: Plato uses fiction extensively while advocating for censorship of fiction. However, this apparent paradox dissolves upon closer examination. Plato does not advocate censoring all fiction, only fiction created by the unwise, particularly poets who lack philosophical knowledge. His own use of myth and narrative is justified, in his view, because philosophers possess the wisdom to wield fiction toward truth. This essay first examines how Plato uses accessible fiction throughout the Republic to spread Socratic ideals to non-philosophical readers. Next, it analyzes how this rhetorical strategy actualizes Athens's fears by corrupting readers away from democratic values through an idealized, fictional Socrates. Finally, it explores Plato's critique of democracy as philosophically unwise, followed by an examination of the fundamental problem in his alternative: his claim that philosopher-kings should rule rests on circular reasoning about who possesses philosophical authority. This essay argues that while Plato successfully validates Athens's fears about rhetoric's corrupting power, he ultimately fails to justify why philosopher-kings are a superior form of governance to democracy.

While Plato advocates for censorship of fiction in the city of Kallipolis, he himself uses fiction throughout the work as a tool to express philosophical thought. The form of the dialogue itself is dramatically engaging, not dry. Plato incorporates stories throughout the work to explain philosophical concepts. The allegory of the cave is used in Book VII to explain philosophical enlightenment, the story of the ring of Gyges in Book II shows a perspective on justice and accountability. The Noble Lie is a myth in two respects: it holds philosophical value in Kallipolis because it is necessary to maintain social organization, but it also serves as a tool to orient both residents of Kallipolis (and readers) towards the truth (Lear 2017, 37). Furthermore, the work of the Republic in its entirety parallels that of an epic. "[the Republic] reveals itself to be a philosophical epic modelled on the plan of Homer's Odyssey," by deploying mythical archetypes of descent to the underworld, dangerous journeys, and returns to reality or homecoming (Howland 2006, 220). This epic structure matters because it shows Plato deliberately chose a narrative form that appeals to the same audience Homer captivated, using the very techniques he claims need to be censored.

Plato's extensive descriptions of what media should or shouldn't be permitted in the city of Kallipolis express strong advocacy for censorship of fiction. In Kallipolis, systematic censorship limits media to that which is virtuous. Gods must be good, heroes shouldn't lament (Plato 2004, 377a ff.). Plato does not intend to censor all fictional works, only those that lack philosophical value or contradict philosophical truth. The Republic itself can be interpreted as a work having such philosophical value, justifying its own use of fiction. Plato doesn't wish to eliminate poetry, but to use it as a tool to demonstrate logical, philosophical arguments: "The language of the poets must resemble the language of the philosopher, who we soon find out, both lies and creates fictions" (Naddaff 2003, 34). The difference between these fictions is that the philosophical ones are "necessary fictions," according to Naddaff, serving philosophy's need to create a separate identity for itself (Naddaff 2003, 7). Plato claims he uses myths after grasping philosophical truth, while poets use them without knowing truth.

It is certainly possible to produce philosophical works without poetic references and structure, so I will now examine why and how Plato uses fiction as a rhetorical device in the Republic. Socrates justifies censorship in Kallipolis on the pretence that it will protect the youth, and states that stories shape souls, especially those of the young who lack the capacity to recognize allegory as such. Myth's ability to foster beliefs means it is an efficient tool to make the less philosophically inclined believe noble things (Plato 2004, 377a ff.). With modern psychology, we can view these statements through a developmental lens. Since children cannot distinguish symbols from reality, they are vulnerable to "paradigms for imitation which, through the imitation, shape the psyche" (Lear 2017, 29). When children hear myths before acquiring the capacity to recognize allegory as such, these stories are "absorbed and retained...taken in as allegories-not-recognized-as-such," creating lasting psychological structures that can either orient or disorient the soul throughout life (Lear 2017, 29).

Given that Plato recognizes fiction's power in influencing the youth and shaping perspective, it is reasonable to deduce that he utilizes fiction in the Republic to these ends. Lear's thesis suggests that myth can be used not merely to illustrate philosophical concepts but to accomplish philosophical work. This reading reveals a crucial insight: the Republic itself enacts the cave allegory's structure. In the allegory, philosophers who achieve enlightenment have a duty to return to the cave and educate those still imprisoned. However, they cannot speak directly of the Forms to prisoners who have only ever seen shadows; they must use the language of shadows (i.e., images and stories) to gradually lead prisoners toward truth. Plato's use of fiction throughout the Republic represents precisely this return to the cave. He cannot present pure dialectic to non-philosophical readers; he must use accessible stories, dramatic dialogues, and compelling myths that readers can use to understand his philosophical perspective. Thus, the Republic is Plato's return to the cave.

It is because of this that the Republic can also be viewed as a revenge for the death of Socrates. It is important to note that Socrates was executed for "corrupting the youth" (Plato 1979, 24b-c), after which Plato created the Republic, a work that has shaped Western thought for millennia, using a rhetorical device that is notably effective at influencing the minds of the youth. By interpreting the Republic as Plato's "return to the cave," we can also view it as a continuation of Socrates's infamous legacy. Furthermore, in the "philosophical epic" of the Republic, Socrates is the main character. While the dialogue form of the Socratic method serves to make the Republic feel like discovery rather than indoctrination, it also allows Plato to write an idealized Socrates who is seemingly endlessly wise, patient, and morally superior.

To consider the Republic a continued vindication or revenge of Socrates is not unreasonable, especially given Plato's denunciation of democracy, the very form of government that led to Socrates's execution. By proposing a philosopher-king rule in the perfectly just city of Kallipolis, Plato demonstrates that democracy killed Socrates because the masses (who elect senators, who voted to kill Socrates) lack the wisdom to distinguish philosophy from sophistry. Because the average person is unenlightened, and leaders of democracy are chosen by popular opinion, democracy gives authority to those unqualified to judge. Therefore, philosopher-kings are the better alternative and only those who know the Form of the Good should rule.

Athens's attempt to enforce traditional values and civic myths by executing Socrates can be seen as their own form of "noble lie." However, Plato distinguishes his censorship from Athens's on philosophical grounds. While Plato advocates noble lies for social cohesion (Myth of Metals) (Plato 2004, 414b-415d) and to aid in understanding truth, he denounces Athens's use of censorship and punishment because it lacks philosophical wisdom. As Naddaff argues, Plato's censorship "increases rather than diminishes the ethical and educational effects" of poetry through "productive" censorship. Philosophy controls poetry's "transgressive qualities" by directing them toward truth rather than merely suppressing dangerous ideas (Naddaff 2003, 25). Democracy's silencing of Socrates was unjust censorship, while the philosopher-kings' city would practice just censorship.

Whether Plato consciously intended the Republic as political commentary is uncertain; he explicitly states that the just city is "a paradigm laid up in heaven," not something that necessarily exists or will exist on earth (Plato 2004, 592a-b). The city may serve primarily as an extended metaphor for the soul. Yet regardless of Plato's intent, the Republic objectively functions as a refutation of Athens's right to judge Socrates and attempts to dismantle many of Athens's core democratic values. Historical context adds greater complexity when we consider that Plato witnessed not only democratic failures but also oligarchic tyranny. Critias, leader of the Thirty Tyrants who brutally ruled Athens in 404-403 BCE, was Socrates' pupil (Mark 2018). Plato experienced both democratic Athens executing his mentor and an oligarchic regime committing atrocities under a Socratic associate. The Republic was written in the context of this political divide which shows neither pure democracy nor tyranny can provide justice.

By examining Plato's use of fiction to promote his philosophical perspective and critique democracy, we find that the Republic proves its own content through its historical success. The work's influence on Western philosophy demonstrates that myth and dialogue can shape beliefs and perspectives across millennia, validating Athens's fear of rhetorical power. Athens was correct that rhetoric can corrupt; the Republic has done precisely this: it incites philosophical thinking in its readers and guides its readers to question democratic values, using accessible dialogue to do so. At the same time, it forms a powerful argument against the kind of government that killed Socrates by highlighting democracy's philosophical inadequacy. The Republic demonstrates that democratic Athens lacked the wisdom to judge which rhetoric serves truth and which corrupts. Democracy could identify rhetoric's danger, but not wield proper philosophical judgment about which ideas deserved suppression. As Howland explicates, the Myth of Er reveals perpetual dissatisfaction with life under non-philosophical regimes, suggesting only philosophy can "save us as we journey through life" (Howland 2006, 223).

The obvious objection to this reading is that Plato is simply being hypocritical by performing the very thing he condemns. However, this misunderstands Plato's position. He denounces the use of myth by those without wisdom; this he views as an injustice. Therefore, philosopher-kings, and Plato himself in the Republic, can use myths justly toward the end of enlightenment, while poets without philosophical knowledge cannot. This distinction between "noble" and "dishonorable" rests entirely on Plato's claim to philosophical authority. Plato's advocacy for censorship in Kallipolis acknowledges that he recognizes fiction's power in creating persuasive philosophical rhetoric, making it unlikely that the coexistence of this alongside his own use of fiction was unintentional.

However, this argument reveals a fundamental problem: who decides which censorship serves truth? If the masses cannot distinguish wisdom from sophistry, how can they recognize that philosopher-kings are truly wise rather than merely clever sophists? Plato's solution, that philosophers must rule, assumes that true philosophers can be identified. Plato's distinction between just and unjust use of fiction (that philosophers may use myths because they grasp philosophical truth, while poets don't) is self-serving. How do we determine who has the authority to decide which lies are "noble"?

Plato attempts to ground this authority in knowledge of the Forms, particularly the Form of the Good (Republic 505a). Only those who can comprehend these eternal truths possess the wisdom to judge which myths serve truth. But this creates an epistemological stalemate: how do non-philosophers verify that someone has genuinely perceived the Forms rather than merely claiming to have done so? The prisoners in the cave cannot judge whether the returning philosopher truly escaped or simply imagined it. Plato's system requires trusting philosophers' self-reports about their access to truth — precisely the unaccountable authority Athens found threatening. Plato argues that only philosophers can judge myth, but he's the one declaring himself a philosopher. Notably, this same concept of unaccountable authority is central to his critique of democracy.

Plato attempts to use fiction to vindicate Socrates, but rather proves Athens was right in its concerns but wrong in its judgment. Athens correctly perceived that rhetoric possesses immense power to shape minds and threaten the existing social order. The Republic's success confirms this: With persuasive, accessible fiction, Plato immortalizes Socrates and corrupts readers away from democratic values and toward philosophical ideals. So, in killing Socrates, Athens failed to silence Socratic thinking. Instead, the execution provoked the Republic, a work that uses the very rhetorical power democracy feared but lacked the philosophical wisdom to properly assess. The Republic thus embodies an unresolvable tension: Plato's success in wielding fiction for philosophical ends validates Athens's fear of rhetorical manipulation, yet this very success depends on readers accepting his unverifiable claim to philosophical authority. Society faces the impossible choice of either granting unaccountable power to self-proclaimed philosophers or risk silencing genuine wisdom with the opinion of an unenlightened populus. Athens chose the latter and killed Socrates; Plato's alternative offers no mechanism to prevent the former. The Republic embodies a great irony: it both validates Athens's fear that rhetoric is powerful and dangerous and exposes democracy's inadequacy.
