What I Write For

April 23rd, 2026

I spent my teenage years, like many of my peers, chasing beauty — trying to define it, recognize it, and produce it. When I began investing a serious amount of my time in writing, my relationship with writing became tangled up with my ambition to make something beautiful.

My most pressing question was what made writing beautiful. It was a quality I struggled to define — there was a point where I became despairingly nihilistic about trying to. Establishing good style felt so subjective. While there are rules for how to write (following conventional spelling, eliminating unnecessary words, applying literary devices), there is no "correct" way to write. The strict rules of grammar and traditional conventions may be daringly broken by the fluid stanzas of poetry. One person may be infatuated by Shakespeare, the next may be enraged by every line of iambic pentameter that fills the page. Even strictly focusing on the works that I found incredibly beautiful, it was difficult to decipher what exactly made me feel that way.

When I settled on a definition of beauty, it was not one limited to writing. And to demonstrate this definition, I find myself not pulling from writing, but from math.

Let's say we wanted to model a population of rabbits to see how the number of rabbits changed over time. We can use a simple formula to model this: x = r × (1 − x), where r is the growth rate constant and x is the number of rabbits. Now let's take a break from the rabbits and move onto something seemingly very different: the Mandelbrot set.

The Mandelbrot set is the set of complex numbers c for which the sequence zn+1 = zn² + c (starting at z0 = 0) does not diverge. Tiny tweaks of parameters reveal intricate, self-similar patterns.

A fractal is just a pattern with large-scale features that are repeated infinitely many times for smaller and smaller scales. Pretty cool, isn't it? But where does it come from? How is it made? And what on earth does it have to do with rabbits?

The Mandelbrot set is defined by the simple quadratic recurrence equation: z = z² + c, where z and c are complex numbers. It turns out that if you run the rabbit population model for hundreds of generations and map how it stabilizes — plotting the long-run behavior against different values of the growth rate — the resulting bifurcation diagram traces the exact same structure as the boundary of the Mandelbrot set. The rabbits were always leading here.

Beautiful ideas emerge from simple and surprising contexts, and they are not only comprehensible but perspective-expanding.

The fractal of the Mandelbrot set itself is nice to look at, but becomes beautiful when you stumble upon it after thinking you were just looking at rabbits. Death becomes beautifully tragic when it is posed against the simplicity of young love. And, I'm not religious, but every mundane object of our daily lives becomes beautiful if you believe it was made by a divine creator (as the mere possibility of the object's existence is illuminated as a grand manifestation of an omnipotent will). This, to me, seems to be a reasonable pattern for approximating what makes something beautiful: the context seems simple, then you get something surprising, and this contrast expands your perspective in some way. I am sure my definition is insufficient (I have not engaged with theories of aesthetics), but it is one that resonates with me.

Producing Beautiful Writing

Beauty is a skill. You can write more beautifully by practicing that skill. You can also write more beautifully by doing the opposite. Literally, do anything but write. Take a walk, observe things and people, travel, read. Do this without expecting anything to come from it. My working hypothesis is that child prodigies in literature are much less common than child prodigies in mathematics because writing requires deep empathy and understanding of the world that you can only garner by experiencing the world.

This even seems to be true for the younger writers I can think of. For example, Mary Shelley wrote Frankenstein at age 19. Still, at this point in her life she experienced the death of her mother, fled the country for an elopement, and had a premature baby who died during childbirth. I don't think you necessarily have to experience great suffering to produce great writing, but the she life experiences she endured before writing Frankenstein undeniably shaped her work. So, part of producing beautiful writing and improving your writing involves emanating your experience of living into your work. Strangely, it might be better for your writing to not write but have rich experiences than it is to lock yourself in a cabin in the woods and waiting for something brilliant to emerge from solitude (or a month-long writing retreat). To have something worth writing about, you must live. And when you sit down to write, remember that the living is the basis of the work.

What I Write For

When I began writing for awards, I tried very hard to create beautiful, brilliant writing. I think part of me struggled with this because youth has the unfortunate side effect of limiting your experiences, but also because I Goodhart's Law'ed on producing beautiful writing instead of writing beautifully as a side effect of expression. This led me to rapidly oscillate between stubbornness (a form of self-defense for my non-brilliantness) and unnecessary self-flagellation. It led me to stop writing.

As this is the first post of a blog I am starting, it is important for me to reflect on why I have not written a long-form piece (minus university assignments) in over two years. I would not like to begin writing again only to hit the same wall that led me to stop. Fortunately, I think I am writing for very different reasons now, so this is unlikely.

In one Astral Codex Ten post, he supposes there are two ways to make people read you: (1) you can write competently about topics that people care about or (2) you can write brilliantly. And, "If you write brilliantly, the world will read your review of watching paint dry. If you're a normal person, you have to content yourself with reaching the limited subset of people who are interested in your topic."

I would like to be humble in how I approach writing this blog and aim toward the latter end: write competently about topics that people care about. But more importantly, I would like to reject the premise. I do not want to write to make people read me. Potentially, my writing will provide some entertainment or insight for others. I will always be glad if my writing finds a reader. However, I am putting to rest the part of me that is scared of The Reader, that is desperate for their attention, that craves and despises their response.

I want to write because it allows me to track my beliefs as they change. I want to write because it creates an external structure for me to clarify and take my own thoughts seriously. I want to write because writing brings me joy and because the ideas I am writing about excite me. That is what I want to write for.
