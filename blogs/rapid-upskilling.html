Rapid Upskilling

July 15th, 2026

I think I was able to upskill pretty quickly in AI Safety during the past year. A lot of this can be attributed to external factors. Your first year of college has much higher expected value for rapid upskilling than some other points in life, as you have a lot of plasticity. Also, as always, some of this is luck: it was luck that the school I happened to go to has a really strong AIS group, it was luck that the people I met were willing to invest time and energy into my growth, it was luck that I was offered access to certain opportunities like OASIS, etc.

But aside from these external factors, I think there are some guiding principles that allowed me to go from not knowing anything about AI Safety to running an AIS group and working from Constellation in <12 months. This advice is probably most applicable to people in my demographic (college undergrads looking to get into AIS) but I think some of this is universal enough for a general audience to appreciate.

My Understanding of Levels of Skill

Notably these don’t have hard boundaries, this is just my loose conceptual understanding about how I think things seem to go. I think it is useful to understand the trajectory you’re trying to follow.

Level 0: Doesn’t know anything about AI safety. Has intellectual curiosity and a strong background or interest in subjects like math, physics, or philosophy.

Level 1: Has done a reading group or extensive reading on their own; understands the motivation of AI safety.

Level 2: Focus reading group or small project, technical or policy. In parallel, you might also at this point be building up some skills in areas like math, computer science, programming, physics, or philosophy.

Level 3: Heavily guided research in a working group where you can easily lean on peers or a research mentor. Maybe you find something interesting, or maybe you gain a more depthful understanding via passive learning.

Level 4: Understands what is compelling or important to work on, then does some work on it. Could still be through a research program or institution with mentorship involved, but high agency is expected. You have a pretty high level of independent thought at this point, and can defend and come up with “takes” on a bunch of different things.

Level 5: Vision for some bigger research agenda; has an idea for how things should go or some theory of change, and tries to get people to follow this theory.

Networking

Networking is everything. Talk to people. There are not many people in AI safety. Also, most people who are mission aligned recognize how high impact it can be to set someone on the path to do this work, so they are likely very willing to talk to you. A good starting point for this for undergrads is to talk to people in the AIS group at your university. 80k can also connect you with someone for career advice. If you go to a conference, schedule 1-on-1s. Also, if you talk to someone, you can ask them “Who do you think I should talk to?” and continue the branches of your network.

When having 1-on-1 conversations, it’s helpful to have some idea of what would make that conversation “successful” for you. What are you pursuing that they can help you with? What unique perspective do they have that would be valuable for you to hear about? If you really have nothing, just asking someone to “say more” when they mention something moderately interesting usually can kick off a better conversation.

Mentors vs Sponsors

I think it's important to have people you look up to or can go to for guidance when trying to upskill. One thing I learned is that there is a distinction between what I’ll call “mentors” and “sponsors.”

Mentorship is actually quite cheap. Lots of people are willing to give you quick feedback about your ideas, or suggest how you might best spend your time.

Sponsors are expensive. Sponsors genuinely care about you doing great things. Sponsors will advocate for you in rooms you’re not in. They will call you out when you’re wrong about something, and they think about how you spend your time even without you asking them to.

Both sponsors and mentors are valuable, but one sponsor is at least worth 10 mentors.

Doing Things

Be agentic but know its limits.

Don’t wait until you’re “ready” to do things, and don’t hesitate to chase an opportunity you don’t feel “ready” for. When I was asked to facilitate our intro to AIS fellowship at UChicago, I did not feel ready. When I was asked to join our exec board, I did not feel ready. When I was offered work from AIFP, I did not feel ready. There will be growing pains, but I think being a little scared of the thing you are about to undertake is a good thing. It is a sign you are pushing yourself. The balance to this is to be forgiving of yourself when you mess up or move at a slower pace than expected.

Don’t Let Speed Kill Your Substance

Undergrad years might be the last time in your life where your primary responsibility is learning things. Rapid upskilling may lead you to end this period of your life earlier than anticipated. Sure, some good learning can come from diving into doing work, but the purity of education can easily be pushed aside as you take on the responsibilities of a “real job.” Especially in a field that is very people-constrained, it is easy to get thrust into a project. Make sure to take time to understand things from a first-principles level as much as you can. Probably be a little more selfish than the field demands of you sometimes. Take time to learn things without feeling like you have to constantly produce output or do the “highest impact” thing. The return on investment is probably worth it.

And I know this is possibly hypocritical, but beware the advice of the 19-year-old founder type who seems to have it “figured out.” They do not. They probably are more likely to lack substance in some areas. Also beware the dangers of deference1.

On the topic of substance, I think this intersects closely with the idea of experts vs elites.

Concluding Thoughts

I think some of the most successful young people in AIS are those with high conscientiousness and some amount of poise. Be kind to others. Be gentle with yourself. It is a hard job trying to save the world. Make sure to put on your oxygen mask before helping others.

1 Don’t be paranoid, but do be skeptical. I overcorrected on being passive by being overly contrarian, which was maybe needed to find that balance, but there’s probably a better way of doing that.
